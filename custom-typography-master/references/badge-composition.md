# Badge Composition Reference

The compositional systems that separate professional badge work from Canva templates.
Every badge is a spatial problem solved with hierarchy, weight, and visual logic.

---

## Containing Shape Archetypes

### Circle / Oval
The most versatile badge form. Self-contained, rotatable, works at any size.
- `perfect circle badge with type running the full perimeter arc`
- `horizontal oval badge with arched headline and straight baseline subtext`
- `circle with outer ring, inner field, and center device`
- `double ring circle badge with decorative rope or chain ring detail`

**Composition rules:**
Type on the top arc reads left-to-right. Type on the bottom arc reads inverted
unless used as a mirror arc (both reading left-to-right requires a baseline flip).
Always specify `top arc reading left-to-right, bottom arc reading left-to-right` when
both arcs have type.

### Shield
Heraldic authority. Works for premium, sports, institutional, and heritage.
- `classic heater shield` — straight top, tapered to point
- `straight-edged shield with rounded bottom corners, no pointed tip` — modern
- `round-top shield` — softer, collegiate feel
- `arrowhead shield pointing down` — aggressive, action sports

**Composition rules:**
Shield works best with 2-3 type zones: headline (top third), device/illustration
(middle third), wordmark or founding date (bottom third).

### Pennant / Banner
Motion and momentum built in. Baseball, vintage sports, travel sticker DNA.
- `triangular pennant shape pointing right` — classic baseball
- `double-tailed ribbon banner, horizontal` — secondary text device
- `arched ribbon banner above or below a central element`
- `waving pennant with subtle perspective`

### Diamond / Lozenge
Less common, more distinctive. Gasoline brand heritage, playing card visual logic.
- `upright diamond shape` — high contrast, minimal text
- `horizontal diamond / lozenge` — wider text accommodation

### Rectangular / Pill
Modern, clean, works for wordmarks and labels.
- `horizontal rectangle with rounded corners` — label and tag aesthetic
- `stadium / pill shape` — sports ticket, travel patch
- `hard-cornered rectangle with border rule` — utilitarian, stencil feel

### Irregular / Hand-drawn Shapes
Badge outlines that feel drawn, not constructed.
- `badge with slightly irregular hand-drawn outline` — craft, imperfect, human
- `wax seal shape` — premium, uneven edge circle
- `patch with stitched edge detail` — woven embroidery aesthetic
- `woodcut badge shape with rough uneven edge`

## Type Zone Systems

### 3-Zone Badge (Classic)

```
[HEADLINE — Primary Brand/Name]
      [CENTRAL DEVICE — Illustration or Icon]
[SUBTEXT — Location, Tagline, or Date]
```

Each zone needs different typographic weight:
- Headline: 50-60% of visual weight
- Central device: 20-30%
- Subtext: 10-20%

**Prompt language:**
`three-zone badge composition: "BRAND NAME" as large condensed headline top third,
central illustration or icon in the middle field, "EST. 2019 / CITY, STATE" as
small-caps secondary type in the bottom third, separated by fine rule lines`

---

### 2-Zone Badge (Strong)

```
[PRIMARY MARK — Large, dominant]
[SECONDARY — Smaller, supporting]
```

The riskier option — but stronger when it works.
- Headline needs to own the space completely
- Secondary can be tiny — the contrast is the design

**Prompt language:**
`two-zone horizontal badge: "BRAND" in ultra-large ultra-condensed black fills the
top 70% of the badge, "SINCE 1987" in tiny small-caps beneath, minimal negative space`

---

### Arc Type Systems

**Full perimeter arc (circle):**
`headline type arching the full top 180 degrees of the circle,
subtext arching the full bottom 180 degrees, centered central device in the field`

**Partial arc (60-90 degrees):**
`headline type in a subtle arc over the central element, not full 180`

**Straight + arc combination:**
`arched headline across the top, straight horizontal subtext at the bottom,
illustration centered in the remaining field`

---

### Divider Devices

Type zones need visual separation. These are the tools:

**Rule lines:**
- `single thin rule, hairline weight, full badge width` — clean division
- `double rule lines flanking secondary text` — traditional, editorial
- `diamond or star ornament on the rule, centered` — decorative break
- `wavy rule line` — vintage, casual
- `dashed rule line` — utilitarian

**Banner ribbons:**
- `small waving ribbon banner holds subtext between headline and illustration`
- `straight ribbon banner at bottom of badge with subtext`

**Ornamental devices:**
- `wheat or laurel sprigs flanking central text` — heritage, award aesthetic
- `five-point star dividers between words` — western, classic Americana
- `bullet points or small diamonds separating elements in subtext line`
- `small crosshatch or asterisk ornaments as word separators`

## Hierarchical Contrast Rules

The single most important thing that separates good badge design from amateur work:
**Every typographic element must have a distinct size and weight identity.**

**Wrong:** `three lines of text all set at similar sizes`
**Right:** `headline 3x the size of the subtext, subtext 2x the size of the footnote`

**Prompt language for correct hierarchy:**
`headline "IRONWORKS" set at maximum scale filling the top of the badge,
"CUSTOM FABRICATION" in condensed caps at 40% of the headline size beneath a rule,
"EST. 1987 — PITTSBURGH, PA" in small-caps at 20% of headline size at the bottom`

## Specific Badge Prompt Templates

### Classic Heritage Badge (full prompt template)
```
Circular heritage badge design. Text reads "BRAND NAME" arching the top 180 degrees,
"EST. 1987" and "CITY, STATE" in small caps along the bottom arc, central illustration
of [DEVICE] in the field. "BRAND NAME" in ultra condensed heavy slab serif with
bracketed serifs, inline hairline detail on main stems. Secondary text in tight
condensed grotesque all-caps. Separated by fine hairline rule lines. Letterpress
ink trap texture, aged paper grain, slightly worn edges. Two-color limited palette:
warm cream ground with deep navy blue type. In the tradition of 1930s-1940s American
heritage commercial lettering. Flat vector graphic on white background.
High-detail boutique design studio quality, no amateur clip art feel. 1:1 ratio.
```

### Athletic Pennant Badge
```
Triangular pennant badge pointing right. Text reads "TEAM NAME" in large
ultra condensed block letters filling the pennant width, "CHAMPIONS" in smaller
condensed caps below, founding year at the pointed tip. Type in bold monoline
condensed gothic all-caps, deliberate tight letterspacing with letters nearly
touching. Faded athletic fabric texture, worn chenille patch feel, screen-print
ink breakdown. Cardinal red pennant with cream type and gold accent outline.
Vintage 1960s collegiate athletic aesthetic. Flat vector graphic.
Production-ready apparel print quality. 4:3 ratio.
```

### Minimal Modern Badge
```
Clean circular badge with outer ring. "BRAND NAME" in condensed bold humanist
sans-serif running the top arc. Central geometric device [DESCRIBE]. "SUBTITLE"
in light weight condensed sans along the bottom arc, tracking wide.
Type in regular geometric sans, even stroke weight, minimal contrast.
Subtle halftone grain on flat color fields. Single-color black on off-white,
risograph print simulation. Modern outdoor brand identity aesthetic,
Pacific Northwest influence. Flat vector graphic, white background.
Sharp boutique design quality. 1:1 ratio.
```

## What Separates Lincoln Design Co from a Template

1. **Every zone has a distinct weight identity.** Not just different sizes — different
   letterform weights, different styles. The contrast is intentional.

2. **Devices are compositionally integrated, not placed.** The illustration and the type
   feel like they were designed together, not stacked independently.

3. **Ornamental devices have visual weight rules.** Thin dividers between heavy type.
   The divider is never competing with the type.

4. **The outer badge shape is part of the design.** The shape responds to the content.
   A circular badge with two lines of arc type is compositionally complete.
   A circle with five lines of text crammed in is not.

5. **Restraint in decoration.** One texture treatment. One or two colors. One style
   of ornamental device. More is dilution.

6. **The type is DESIGNED, not placed.** Specific letterform decisions, not "bold serif."
