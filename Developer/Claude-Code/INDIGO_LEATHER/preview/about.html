The Beginning

A coming together of past and present.

In high school, I took fashion design courses and interned with a leather designer. Years later, after a career in the corporate world, my husband’s late grandfather passed away and left behind a leather working set. Something clicked.

I dove in: first tooling, then bag construction, then refinement — learning and synthesizing until I found my own approach. Not western. Not artisan-rustic. Somewhere between a fashion house and a craftsperson’s workshop.

The Indigo Woman is the customer I keep returning to in my mind. A woman with quiet confidence who buys what she genuinely loves, not what everyone else is carrying. If the bag is beautiful and well-made, that’s enough. That’s the work.

115 steps. Every bag.

Marking the pattern. Cutting. Interfacing. Gluing. Burnishing. Setting hardware. Sewing. Tamping. Through every subassembly and into the final bag. The decisions embedded in the process — leather stiffness for bag type, stitch prominence in different leathers, edge finish technique (French fold vs burnished raw edge) — are not afterthoughts. They are the design.

Some leathers will collapse when you try to burnish them. In a stiffer leather, the stitch sits proud. In a softer one, it nestles into the surface. These are not technical details for technicians — they’re the decisions that determine whether a bag looks expensive or just looks handmade.
